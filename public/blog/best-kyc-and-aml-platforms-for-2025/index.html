Regional considerations

United States

The US regulatory framework includes the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), the USA PATRIOT Act and the AML Act 2020. FinCEN enforces customer due diligence rules requiring verification of beneficial owners and ongoing transaction monitoring. When choosing a platform, ensure it supports US watchlists, 314(a) information sharing and currency transaction report (CTR) filing. Providers like ComplyAdvantage and Veridaq maintain strong US coverage, while European‚Äëcentric platforms may require additional data feeds.

United Kingdom

The UK‚Äôs Money Laundering Regulations and FCA guidance require firms to perform customer due diligence, enhanced due diligence for high‚Äërisk customers and ongoing monitoring. The regulator also emphasises risk‚Äëbased approaches and robust record‚Äëkeeping. Providers with FCA registration or UK‚Äëspecific databases (e.g. Companies House checks) are advantageous. Veriff and iDenfy offer broad UK document support, while TTMS focuses more on EU markets[3].

European Union

The EU‚Äôs 6th Anti‚ÄëMoney‚ÄëLaundering Directive (6AMLD) and the upcoming Anti‚ÄëMoney‚ÄëLaundering Authority (AMLA) mandate stricter penalties and harmonised compliance across Member States. Institutions must screen against EU sanctions lists and beneficial ownership registers. Platforms like Vespia and TTMS integrate with EU databases[3]. Organisations should confirm that providers comply with GDPR and have data‚Äëprocessing agreements in place.

Frequently asked questions

What‚Äôs the difference between KYC and AML?

KYC (Know Your Customer) refers to the process of verifying a customer‚Äôs identity at onboarding and ensuring that they are who they claim to be. AML (Anti‚ÄëMoney‚ÄëLaundering) encompasses ongoing monitoring of customer behaviour, screening against sanctions and PEP lists and detecting suspicious transactions. Both are complementary‚ÄîKYC is the first step in a broader AML programme.

Do I need separate tools for KYC and AML?

Not necessarily. Some platforms combine KYC and AML into a single suite (e.g. Veridaq), while others specialise in one area. Integrated tools simplify vendor management and data flow, but specialised tools can offer depth in a particular domain. Assess your compliance requirements, integration capabilities and budget to decide whether an all‚Äëin‚Äëone or best‚Äëof‚Äëbreed approach suits you.

How do free trials work?

Many vendors offer free trials or sandboxes where you can test the API and user interface. For example, Vespia lists a free trial for its AML software[9], and Sanction Scanner offers a trial for its screening tools. Trials typically limit the number of checks or data sources but help you assess integration effort and user experience.

What data privacy standards should a provider meet?

A credible provider should comply with data protection laws such as GDPR (for EU residents) and CCPA (for California). Look for encryption at rest and in transit, secure data‚Äëstorage practices and clear data‚Äëretention policies. Many platforms also undergo independent security audits.

How do sanctions, PEP and adverse media screening differ?

¬∑       Sanctions screening checks customers against lists of individuals and entities subject to government‚Äëimposed restrictions (e.g. OFAC, EU sanctions).

¬∑       PEP screening identifies politically exposed persons and their relatives/associates, who pose higher corruption risks.

¬∑       Adverse media screening searches news and media sources for negative information about an individual or business.
Effective AML software combines these checks with risk scoring and ongoing monitoring[14].
